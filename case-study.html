Spoon Tracker: Building the Tool I Actually Needed

I was between jobs and had been sick for months. I used that time to build the energy tracker I couldn't find anywhere else, with AI writing the code. Ninety days of living in it showed me what was missing. So I kept building. Still am.

BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

What I Did, and Why It Matters Twice

I built an energy-tracking app for my hereditary motor neuropathy, lived in it for 90 days, then audited my real calendar against its presets. The audit showed the app was blind to a third of my life. I shipped 17 new activity presets and 3 new categories, with AI writing the code, without breaking a single existing log entry. Daily "custom entry" workarounds dropped to almost none. The result matters on two levels:

Personal impact

I use this app every day to decide what my body can afford. It surfaced energy I was spending without noticing: showers, paperwork, phone calls, travel days. Because of what it showed me, I now spread appointments out instead of batching them, count massage as recovery instead of cost, and plan around a flight like the major expense it is. It's not a side project. It's infrastructure.

Professional impact

This is my research practice applied to the hardest participant to study honestly: myself. Behavioral data over self-report, taxonomy design, tiered prioritization, and one hard ship constraint (don't break months of logged data). AI wrote every line of code. I scoped the problems, wrote the prompts, caught the mistakes, and shipped. Those are skills I'm building on purpose, and this project is where I build them.

Background

I Needed a Tool That Didn't Exist

I first heard about spoon theory from a counselor when I lived in DC, about eight years ago. The idea stuck with me — you start each day with a limited number of spoons, every activity costs one, and when they're gone, they're gone. Running out mid-afternoon doesn't mean you're tired. It means you might not be able to cook dinner, take a shower, or hold a conversation. It requires actual planning, not just willpower.

I've been working through a diagnosis for over a decade. Full genomic sequencing at NIH finally gave me an answer: Charcot-Marie-Tooth Type 2U, from a variant on the MARS1 gene. It felt like the thing I had been searching for my entire life. Then an aunt with the same disease tested negative for MARS1, and we were back to square one. My neurologist at Johns Hopkins calls it hereditary motor neuropathy, because that's what we know for certain. I identify closely with the CMT2 community because the pattern looks like theirs. For most of my 20s and early 30s the neuropathy was there, but I could outrun it. Not anymore. A full day of appointments, a flight, a grocery run: these have real consequences now. Pacing myself isn't something I ever thought I'd need to do, and then suddenly it was.

From December through April I was on medical leave, sick in a way I hadn't been for a while. Months in bed. Around the third month I knew I needed two things to change. My body needed an actual system, not a mental note. And I needed to stop watching AI tools improve from the sidelines and build something real with them. The app became both at once: the pacing tool my body needed, and hands-on practice building with AI. I'm still learning. I expect I will be for a while.

I looked for an app that tracked energy the way I actually needed. I didn't find one. So I built one: spoon-tracker, on GitHub Pages. I'm a UX researcher, not a developer. I used Claude to write the code. This isn't a case study about building the app. It's about what happened after I'd been living in it for 90 days and realized it still wasn't seeing my whole life.

The Problem

The Tracker Couldn't See My Whole Day

When I built the app, I started with 17 activity presets — the obvious stuff: PT, CMT class, grocery shopping, cooking, walks with my dog Charlie, sleep. That felt like enough at the time. It wasn't.

Pretty quickly I noticed I was hitting "custom entry" constantly. OT exercises every single morning — no preset. Phone calls with friends every week — no preset. Job search work eating up real cognitive energy — no category for it at all. And at some point I realized I had started putting "Take Shower" on my calendar, and that it had nowhere to go in the app either. That one stopped me cold.

The thing about designing for yourself: The presets you build at the start reflect what you think you do. Ninety days of actual use shows you what you actually do. The gap between those two things is what this is about.

Research Questions

What I Needed to Figure Out

  • What am I actually doing every week that the app has no preset for — and how often is it happening?

  • Are there patterns in how I use energy (stacking activities, calendar noise, things that feel similar but cost differently) that the app isn't capturing?

  • What should I add first, and what should each new activity cost?

How I Did It

90 Days of Calendar as Data

I didn't run a usability study on myself. What I did was look at my Google Calendar — 90 days of it, March through June 2026 — and compare every recurring event against the app's 17 presets to see what wasn't covered.

Why the calendar and not the app logs? Because the app logs only show what the presets let me log easily. If there's no preset for OT exercises, those entries either go in as "custom" or they don't get logged at all — either way, the log misses them. My calendar captured my actual days, not just the days my app was designed to see.

  • 1

    Pull everything

    Went through 90 days of calendar events and catalogued every recurring activity by name, how often it showed up, and roughly what it costs me.

  • 2

    Find the gaps

    Compared each calendar event to the 17 existing presets. Anything without a good match got flagged.

  • 3

    Assign costs from experience

    For each gap, I estimated a spoon cost based on how those activities actually feel — not a formula, just lived knowledge. Then sorted them into zones.

  • 4

    Look at the bigger picture

    Asked whether the gaps were individual missing presets or whether whole categories of my life were unaccounted for. (They were.)

  • 5

    Prioritize and build

    Sorted everything into tiers by frequency and impact, then worked with AI to write and ship the code changes.

A limitation worth naming: Calendar data captures what you planned, not what happened. The daily 6:30 AM workout is on the calendar every day — I don't always do it. App logs would show the real frequency. The calendar can't tell the difference between "did this" and "meant to do this."

The Shower Finding

I was scheduling "Take Shower" in my calendar. Not as a preference or a reminder. As a necessity — to make sure I didn't forget it, or run out of energy to do it before the day ended.

— Week 3 of the study period

This is the one that made me stop and sit with it for a while. A shower isn't just a missing preset. It's a thing healthy people never think about and my neuropathy makes me plan for. The app had rest for downtime and recovery for restorative credit, but nothing for the basic cost of maintaining a functional human body — showering, getting dressed, hygiene. Those things cost spoons. They were just invisible.

Adding a self-care category wasn't a taxonomy cleanup. It was the app finally being honest about what my days actually involve.

Other Things I Noticed

Missing presets weren't the only problem. There were patterns in how my days were structured that the app didn't have a way to handle.

⚡

Activity Stacking

Morning blocks frequently combined Mastermind Meeting with Team Platypus/Training. Stacking amplifies depletion beyond the sum of parts — but only gets caught when all activities are logged.

📅

Calendar Noise

Some shared calendar events (Dan's soccer, Dan's yard work) weren't mine. On a shared calendar these could create false positive activity data.

🐕

Granularity Matters

Dog walk and Charlie playtime aren't the same thing. One involves physical exertion; the other is closer to recovery. One preset misrepresents both.

💆

Recovery Is Active

Massage isn't neutral. It was logging as a cost rather than a credit. Passive rest and active recovery need to be treated differently.

Shipping It

From Findings to Live App

I worked with Claude to write the code changes — one clean update to DEFAULT_ACTIVITIES and DEFAULT_ZONES in app.js. The only hard constraint was that all 17 original activity IDs had to stay exactly as they were. Everything I'd already logged lived in localStorage keyed to those IDs. Renaming anything would have broken months of real data. So the update was purely additive: 17 new presets, nothing touched that was already there.

Three new categories got added to the UI — work, travel, self-care — with new color styling. Quick-access defaults on the Today screen got updated from the arbitrary initial set to the five activities I actually log most. One commit, pushed to main, GitHub Pages rebuilt automatically.

No regressions. Commit message: "Expand default activities based on 90-day calendar analysis." All existing log entries still intact. The app still knows about every spoon I spent before the update.

Reflection

What Worked, What Didn't, What I'd Do Differently

Using calendar data instead of app logs was the right call. App logs feel like the natural source — they're structured, timestamped, already in the system. But they only show what your presets let you see. The calendar saw my whole day regardless of whether the app was ready for it.

Being the only participant in your own study is a real validity problem and I'm not going to pretend otherwise. Every spoon cost I assigned is a judgment call based on how things feel, not a measured value. My 1-spoon shower might be someone else's 3-spoon shower. There's no external calibration here. But for a personal health tool, lived knowledge from the inside is actually the right data. Trying to make it more "objective" would make it less accurate for the person it's built for.

What I'd do differently: From day one, I'd log custom entries separately with the raw text typed. That would have surfaced gaps automatically instead of requiring a manual calendar comparison — and made this kind of audit much faster the next time.

The thing I keep coming back to: behavioral data and what you think you do are almost never the same thing. I built this app because I thought I knew what I was spending my energy on. Ninety days of data showed me I was missing a third of it.

Phase 2 — Zone Intelligence

The Framework I'd Already Built Without Knowing It

I recently took over as Moderator for my Never Search Alone Job Search Council, a small group of people who've been in each other's corners through a tough job market. After one of our meetings, I ran the transcript through Granola.ai. The AI summary pointed me toward Gay Hendricks' four zones from The Big Leap, and I realized I'd already built something that looked almost exactly like it. The app had zones from the start: maintenance, competence, excellence, genius. I'd designed them from what felt true about how activities land differently. The Hendricks framework gave me language for what I'd built intuitively, and surfaced one important gap I'd missed.

Five Zones (the Four Hendricks + One I Had to Add)

🔴

Zone of Incompetence

Activities someone else should do entirely. Flagged with a gentle prompt: "Could someone else do this?" Full spoon cost.

⚪

Zone of Competence

Functional, unremarkable. You can do it, but it doesn't energize you. Errands, Slack checks. Full spoon cost.

🟠

Zone of Excellence

You're great at it, which is why people keep asking — but it quietly drains you. Cost +0.5 to surface the hidden drain.

🟢

Zone of Genius

Time disappears. This is the target. 50% of normal spoon cost, because it gives back. The goal: more of the week here.

🔵

Zone of Maintenance

The fifth zone — added because my situation required it. PT, OT, SLP, CMT class. Not leaks. Infrastructure. Protected, tracked separately, never penalized.

The Asterisk That Generic Advice Misses

Granola surfaced a rule-of-thumb: if more than 20% of your week is in Incompetence + Competence zones, you're leaking energy. I get why that works for most people. But Granola also labeled "medical appointments" as Zone of Incompetence. That framing doesn't work for me.

PT, OT, SLP, CMT class — these aren't leaks. They're the reason I can do anything else. Any advice built for an able-bodied baseline needs this asterisk. The Maintenance zone is that asterisk — visible but never penalized.

Granola also suggested batching appointments. I already know what that feels like: one Thursday I had Doctor at 7 AM, PT at 9:30, CMT class at 11. Felt efficient. Required a 2-hour nap. Spreading appointments out feels less tidy — but it's actually how I protect the rest of the day.

What's Actually in My Zone of Genius

The Granola conversation surfaced a question I hadn't asked myself directly: what am I doing when I lose track of time?

Building playlists for specific people. Reorganizing files and folders for someone else. Researching and reaching back out to people from my past. I go into flow when I'm creating structure or making a connection in service of another person. That's mine — and it's also a design input. The Zone of Genius tracker isn't just motivating. It's the actual measure of whether the pacing is working.

Status: Ongoing

This Is a Living Thing

The app gets updated when real life shows me something's missing. That's how Phase 1 happened. That's how Phase 2 happened. There's no finished version, just the version that's most accurate right now.

I want to be honest about what this project has been for me beyond the pacing. I never thought I would vibe-code anything. But I asked Claude to write the code, iterated on it, pushed it to GitHub, and now I use the result every day. That's been the most useful way I've stayed current with what AI tools can actually do. Not by reading about it. By building something real that matters to me.

A concrete example of how that iteration works: the app was defaulting to UTC for all date handling, so my "today" sometimes rolled over at the wrong time and the 5:15 AM check-in wasn't firing at 5:15 Eastern. It took living in the app to notice. One conversation to diagnose it, one Claude Code prompt, one commit: America/New_York hard-coded as the default. My pacing is anchored to my home routine, not UTC. That's the kind of thing you don't spec up front. You find it by using the thing.

I'm not done. There are things in the current version I already know I want to test; some will stick, some won't. The app will keep changing as long as I keep using it. It's the tool I needed for myself. That's why I built it. And it keeps getting better because I keep using it, which is how any good tool should work.
